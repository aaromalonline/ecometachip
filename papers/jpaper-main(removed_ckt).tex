% --- Abstract ---
\noindent\textbf{Abstract---}This paper presents the Ecometachip, an innovative, low-cost, and eco-friendly electronic system designed for the preliminary identification of common liquids (water, ethanol, coconut oil, and petrol) based on variations in their relative permittivity ($\epsilon_r$). While Coir-Rubber Composites (CoRC) are traditionally synthesized as high-efficiency radio wave absorbers through the macroscopic dissipation of electromagnetic energy via carbon fillers, this work mathematically re-engineers the material into a near-field coupled secondary dielectric resonator. By integrating a 2 mm porous CoRC patch into a non-contact 100 MHz Hartley oscillator circuit, liquid absorption displaces internal air voids, fundamentally altering the complex permittivity of the matrix and modulating the coupled magnetic field amplitude. Extensive hardware experimentation and automated Python-based statistical analysis confirm high system linearity, demonstrating a sensitivity of 2.31 mV/$\epsilon_r$ ($R^2 = 0.906$) across diverse dielectric fluids. Rigorous environmental robustness characterization over multiple test sessions reveals exceptional stability, with thermal drift constrained to 0.50 mV/$^\circ$C and humidity drift limited to 0.04 mV/\%RH. The architecture successfully drives a multi-level LED visual classification, establishing a reproducible, resource-constrained foundation for future machine-learning-based digital chemical sensing.

\section{Introduction}
The rapid, accurate, and cost-effective identification of common liquids is a critical necessity across environmental monitoring, fuel quality control, petrochemical refining, and laboratory analysis. Traditional methodologies heavily rely on spectroscopic (e.g., benchtop FT-IR) or chromatographic techniques. While highly accurate, these systems require expensive instrumentation, complex sample preparation, specialized operator training, and significant power consumption. Addressing the urgent need for low-cost, biodegradable sensing suitable for resource-constrained field analysis, this work introduces the Eco-MetaChip Mini---a dielectric-based chemical identifier \cite{b4}.

The fundamental operational principle relies on the relationship between a target liquid's relative permittivity ($\epsilon_r$) and its loading effect on a high-frequency electromagnetic field. The primary novelty of this system lies in the total replacement of traditional FR4 PCB substrates with an eco-friendly Coir-Rubber Composite (CoRC) patch. Originally investigated by researchers at the Cochin University of Science and Technology (CUSAT) to create ``radiation-free zones'' via highly efficient radio wave absorption, this biodegradable matrix of coconut pith fibers, natural rubber latex, and charcoal powder \cite{b7} possesses unique, frequency-selective electromagnetic properties \cite{b1, b2}.

This paper bridges a critical gap between microwave absorption theory and near-field dielectric sensing. It provides a comprehensive, mathematically rigorous methodology that transitions the CoRC from a macroscopic wave absorber to a microstructural dielectric resonator. The study is validated through LTspice circuit simulations, detailed hardware implementations, comprehensive empirical observation logs, and automated Python-driven data analysis to mathematically prove sensor linearity, reproducibility, and environmental repeatability.

\section{Theoretical Framework and Mathematical Modeling}

\subsection{Macroscopic Electromagnetic Interaction in CoRC}
The sensing substrate is derived from an agricultural by-product composite consisting of coconut pith powder, natural rubber latex, and charcoal powder (carbon) \cite{b7}. In macroscopic electromagnetic theory, the interaction of a radio wave with any given medium depends on its complex permittivity ($\epsilon^*$) and complex permeability ($\mu^*$) \cite{b8}. Because agricultural by-products and carbon fillers lack significant metallic composition, their magnetic properties are negligible ($\mu_r \approx 1$), and the material behaves purely as a complex dielectric \cite{b8}:
\begin{equation}
    \epsilon^* = \epsilon' - j\epsilon''
\end{equation}
The real part ($\epsilon'$) represents electric energy storage capacity, whereas the imaginary part ($\epsilon''$) is associated with dielectric loss---the energy dissipated as heat within the material \cite{b8}. The effectiveness of the material in interacting with and attenuating electromagnetic waves is governed by its loss tangent ($\tan \delta_\epsilon$) and its attenuation coefficient ($\alpha$) \cite{b8}:
\begin{equation}
    \tan \delta_\epsilon = \frac{\epsilon''}{\epsilon'}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \alpha = \frac{\sqrt{2}\pi f}{v} \sqrt{\sqrt{({\mu'}^2{\epsilon'}^2 + {\mu'}^2{\epsilon''}^2)} - \mu'\epsilon'}
\end{equation}
Because the carbon/charcoal additive is a highly conductive medium, standard thick formulations of CoRC act as exceptional microwave absorbents \cite{b1}. As high-frequency waves propagate through the material, surface currents generate heat, dissipating the electromagnetic energy \cite{b1}. The total absorptivity ($A$) is verified via the linear power conservation equation utilizing transmission ($S_{21}$) and reflection ($S_{11}$) coefficients \cite{b1}:
\begin{equation}
    A = 1 - |S_{11}|^2 - |S_{21}|^2
\end{equation}
In standard planar configurations backed by a conductor, a 6 mm thick CoRC achieves up to 74\% absorptivity in the X-band (8.2–12 GHz), effectively trapping the signal \cite{b1}. 

\subsection{Microstructural Permittivity Shift: The Resonator Paradigm}
To transition the CoRC from a pure signal trap (absorber) to an active near-field sensor, the material thickness is strictly reduced to 2 mm. At this thickness, the macroscopic volume is insufficient to entirely dissipate the 100 MHz wave. Furthermore, Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) reveals that the addition of carbon renders the CoRC matrix highly porous, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:sem_image} \cite{b1}. 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{sem_image.png}
    \caption{Scanning electron microscopy of (a) CoR and (b) CoRC substrates. The addition of carbon to the base CoR matrix creates the highly porous CoRC micro-structure utilized by the Eco-MetaChip for fluid integration. Image reproduced from Manohar et al. \cite{b1}.}
    \label{fig:sem_image}
\end{figure}

While these pores traditionally introduce multiple boundary reflections that increase power loss \cite{b1}, the Eco-MetaChip leverages this exact porosity for liquid integration. When a 50 $\mu$L liquid droplet is applied to the surface, capillary action wicks the fluid into the matrix, violently displacing the internal air voids (where air $\epsilon_r \approx 1$). This volumetric displacement by a fluid (such as water, $\epsilon_r \approx 80$ \cite{b3}) radically shifts the effective real permittivity ($\epsilon'_{eff}$) of the coupled system \cite{b5, b6}, fundamentally altering its capacitance without short-circuiting the underlying electronics.

\subsection{Near-Field Dielectric Loading and Oscillator Dynamics}
The Eco-MetaChip generates a continuous 100 MHz RF magnetic field utilizing a Hartley oscillator topology. The self-resonant frequency ($f_o$) of the primary LC tank is dictated by:
\begin{equation}
    f_o \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{LC_{eff}}}
\end{equation}
The CoRC patch functions as a dielectrically loaded secondary resonator. An increase in the substrate's $\epsilon_r$ directly increases the effective mutual capacitance ($C_{eff}$) of the near-field environment. According to Equation 5, this forces a downward shift in the resonant frequency, which subsequently modulates the amplitude of the resultant fluctuating magnetic field ($B_{RF}$). This amplitude-modulated field is captured non-contactively by a secondary pickup coil located precisely 2 mm away.

\section{System Design and Hardware Architecture}
\subsection{Sensor Fabrication and Component Specifications}
The CoRC patch is fabricated using a precise ratio of 60\% coir fiber, 30\% nitrile rubber, and 10\% carbon-black with Copper (Cu) filler. The composite is hydraulically pressed into a 30 mm $\times$ 30 mm patch with a uniform thickness of 2 mm. The complete hardware prototype was assembled on a standard 830-point breadboard. \subsection{Circuit Implementation and LTSpice Modeling}
Prior to physical deployment, the electronic architecture was mathematically modeled using LTspice. The Hartley oscillator utilizes a 47 pF NPO ceramic capacitor and a hand-wound 15-turn toroidal inductor ($54\text{ nH}$). 

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{ampcircuit.png}

    \label{fig:ltspice_circuit}
\end{figure}

The non-contact inductive link between the primary toroid and the secondary pickup coil is modeled using variable coupling coefficients (\texttt{k\_val}). As defined in Table \ref{tab:coupling}, \texttt{k\_val} mathematically scales with the physical relative permittivity ($\epsilon_r$) of the fluid absorbed by the CoRC substrate.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Simulation Parameters: Mathematical \texttt{k\_val} mapped to Physical Permittivity ($\epsilon_r$)}
\label{tab:coupling}
\begin{tabular}{|l|c|l|}
\hline
\textbf{Coupling Coefficient (\texttt{k\_val})} & \textbf{Relative Permittivity ($\epsilon_r$)} & \textbf{Physical Significance (Sample Liquid)} \\ \hline
0 & 0 & No coupling (Ideal Isolation) \\ \hline
0.2 & 1 & Very Weak Link (Air / Empty Sensor) \\ \hline
0.4 & 1.9 -- 3.0 & Standard Operating Range (Petrol / Oils) \\ \hline

\end{tabular}
\end{table}

To extract the DC representation of the modulated RF signal, a precision bridge rectifier is required. Standard silicon diodes suffer from junction capacitance issues at 100 MHz; therefore, fast-recovery 1N5711 Schottky diodes were selected for the hardware prototype to efficiently rectify the signal before it is smoothed by a 100 $\mu$F filter capacitor.

\subsection{Automated Statistical Data Analysis}
To ensure the absolute mathematical reproducibility of the sensitivity and environmental characterizations, an automated data pipeline was developed using Python within a Jupyter Notebook environment. The \texttt{pandas} library was utilized for data structuring, while \texttt{scipy.stats.linregress} was deployed to compute exact environmental drift coefficients and system linearity parameters (Slope and $R^2$), eliminating manual calculation bias. The comprehensive analytical codebase used to derive the study's conclusions is provided in Listing \ref{lst:env_analysis} and \ref{lst:sense_analysis}.

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Python implementation for computing Environmental Robustness and Drift characteristics.}, label=lst:env_analysis]
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import seaborn as sns
from scipy import stats
import numpy as np

plt.rcParams.update({'font.size': 15})

# 1. Load your dataset
df = pd.read_csv('complete_preampobslog.csv')
df.columns = ['Date', 'Humidity', 'Temp', 'Sample', 'Vdc'] # Normalize names
df['Session'] = df['Date'] + " (H:" + df['Humidity'].astype(str) + "%)"
df_temp = df.dropna(subset=['Temp'])

# 2. Setup Figure
plt.style.use('seaborn-v0_8-whitegrid')
fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(16, 7))

# --- Subplot 1: Humidity Robustness ---
sns.pointplot(data=df, x='Sample', y='Vdc', hue='Session', 
              linestyles='none', dodge=0.5, palette='viridis', ax=ax1)
ax1.set_title('Output Consistency across Variable Humidity', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax1.set_ylabel(r'Pre-Amplification Output ($V_{dc}$ in mV)')

# Calculate and add Humidity Coefficient
h_slope, _, _, _, _ = stats.linregress(df['Humidity'], df['Vdc'])
ax1.text(0.05, 0.95, fr'Drift: {h_slope:.2f} mV/%RH', transform=ax1.transAxes, 
         bbox=dict(facecolor='white', alpha=0.8), verticalalignment='top')

# --- Subplot 2: Thermal Stability ---
sns.lineplot(data=df_temp, x='Temp', y='Vdc', hue='Sample', 
             marker='o', markersize=8, linewidth=2, palette='Set1', ax=ax2)
ax2.set_title(r'Thermal Stability Analysis: $V_{dc}$ vs. Temp', fontsize=12, fontweight='bold')
ax2.set_xlabel(r'Ambient Temperature ($^\circ$C)')

# Calculate and add Thermal Coefficient
t_slope, _, _, _, _ = stats.linregress(df_temp['Temp'], df_temp['Vdc'])
ax2.text(0.05, 0.95, fr'Drift: {t_slope:.2f} mV/$^\circ$C', transform=ax2.transAxes, 
         bbox=dict(facecolor='white', alpha=0.8), verticalalignment='top')

# Formatting
plt.suptitle('Eco-Metachip Environmental Robustness Characterization (Repeatability)', fontsize=16, fontweight='bold', y=0.98)
ax1.legend(title='Test Conditions', loc='upper center', bbox_to_anchor=(0.5, -0.15), ncol=2)
ax2.legend(title='Liquid Sample', loc='upper center', bbox_to_anchor=(0.5, -0.15), ncol=2)
plt.tight_layout(rect=[0, 0.05, 1, 0.95])
plt.savefig('env_repeatability_analysis.png', dpi=300)
plt.show()
\end{lstlisting}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=Python, caption={Python implementation for evaluating Sensor Sensitivity and Linearity Regression.}, label=lst:sense_analysis]
# 1. Load the data
df = pd.read_csv('general_preampobs.csv')
df.columns = ['Sample', 'Dielectric', 'Mean', 'Min', 'Max']

# 2. Extract variables
X = df['Dielectric'].values
Y = df['Mean'].values
Y_err_min = Y - df['Min'].values
Y_err_max = df['Max'].values - Y

# 3. Perform Linear Regression
slope, intercept, r_value, p_value, std_err = stats.linregress(X, Y)

# 4. Create the Plot
plt.style.use('seaborn-v0_8-whitegrid')
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 7))

# Plot experimental points with error bars
plt.errorbar(X, Y, yerr=[Y_err_min, Y_err_max], fmt='o', color='darkred', 
             ecolor='lightcoral', elinewidth=3, capsize=5, label='Experimental Data')

# Plot Trendline - fr string allows both {variables} and \latex symbols
x_fit = np.linspace(X.min(), X.max(), 100)
y_fit = slope * x_fit + intercept
plt.plot(x_fit, y_fit, 'b--', alpha=0.7, label=fr'Sensitivity Fit: {slope:.2f} mV/$\epsilon_r$')

# 5. ANNOTATE LIQUIDS
for i, txt in enumerate(df['Sample']):
    plt.annotate(txt, (X[i], Y[i]), xytext=(8, -5), textcoords='offset points', 
                 ha='left', fontsize=10, fontweight='bold')

# 6. Formatting for APSCON
plt.title(r'Eco-Metachip Sensitivity Analysis ($V_{dc}$ vs. $\epsilon_r$)', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold')
plt.xlabel(r'Dielectric Constant ($\epsilon_r$)', fontsize=12)
plt.ylabel(r'Pre-Amplification Output ($V_{dc}$ in mV)', fontsize=12)

# Stats Box - THE FIX: Using fr-string here solves the SyntaxWarning
stats_text = (fr'Sensitivity: {slope:.2f} mV/$\epsilon_r$' + '\n' + 
              fr'$R^2$ Score: {r_value**2:.3f}')

plt.text(45, 100, stats_text, fontsize=11, bbox=dict(facecolor='white', alpha=0.8, edgecolor='gray'))

plt.show()

\subsection{Environmental Robustness and Repeatability}
For any field-deployable analytical instrument, immunity to environmental fluctuations is paramount. Utilizing the extensive dataset provided in Table \ref{tab:completelog}, the Python pipeline computed the baseline output drift across massive environmental swings.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Variable Humidity (Left Panel, Figure \ref{fig:robustness}):} Evaluated across severe relative humidity (RH) variations ranging from an arid 32\% to a saturated 86\%, the calculated baseline drift was exceptionally low at \textbf{0.04 mV/\%RH}.

These robustness characteristics prove the system successfully maintains distinct voltage separation bounds between chemical samples, regardless of standard atmospheric interference.

In physical operation, the forward voltage drops ($V_f$) of parallel Red, Green, and Blue LEDs serve as an analog voltage discriminator:
\begin{itemize}
    \item A low-$\epsilon_r$ liquid like Petrol (yielding a mean of 72 mV) only weakly biases the base of the driver, dimly illuminating the \textbf{Red LED}.
    \item Ethanol ($\epsilon_r \approx 24$, yielding 184 mV) provides enough current gain to trigger both the \textbf{Red and Green LEDs}.
    \item Water, generating the maximum 262 mV baseline due to its massive $\epsilon_r \approx 80$, successfully triggers the highest-threshold \textbf{Blue LED} alongside Red and Green.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Scope: MCU Integration and Digital Classification}
While the analog LED classification system provides rapid, low-cost field analysis, the future architecture of the Ecometachip focuses on high-precision digital output. A hardware-to-digital bridge is currently under development to process the 0--300 mV analog $V_{dc}$ signal.

This proposed architecture utilizes a non-inverting operational amplifier to scale the raw millivolt output up to a standard 3.3V logic level, coupled with a DC offset clamper circuit to strictly ensure a positive baseline, protecting the subsequent Microcontroller Unit (MCU). The MCU's Analog-to-Digital Converter (ADC) will digitize the scaled signal. Once digitized, an embedded Machine Learning classification engine---prototyped using Scikit-Learn (sklearn) Python models trained on the exact relative permittivity vs. $V_{dc}$ variations mapped in this study---will be deployed. This will enable the device to digitally classify liquids, outputting specific chemical names to an LCD or IoT pipeline rather than relying on LED thresholding.

\section{Conclusion}
The Ecometachip successfully establishes a mathematically rigorous, highly linear, and remarkably stable system for liquid chemical identification. By radically re-engineering the complex permittivity properties of an eco-friendly coir-rubber-carbon composite---transitioning it from a macroscopic radio wave absorber into a highly porous, near-field dielectric resonator---the system exhibits a robust sensitivity of 2.31 mV/$\epsilon_r$. Furthermore, comprehensive multi-session empirical logging proves the sensor possesses excellent environmental repeatability, establishing a sustainable, resource-constrained foundation for the future of ubiquitous chemical sensing.
